Who Owns
AI-Commerce?

By Tendai Mudavanhu · Melbourne, Australia

In July 2024, at a Melbourne Business School AI Challenge run by Microsoft, I proposed an idea that won the competition: a complete abstraction of the computer into just a text and voice prompt. No apps, no browsers, no storefronts. Just a black box you speak into, eventually replaced entirely by wearables. Every phone reduced to Siri and a prompt window. Everything else handled by agents.

Eighteen months later, we are almost there. AI agents are becoming the primary interface between people and businesses. A customer will not visit a retailer's website. They will tell an agent what they need, and the agent will find it, compare prices, and buy it. I call this AI-commerce: the next generation of commercial interaction, mediated entirely by agents.

In this world, there are no websites to fall back on. No apps to browse. The agent decides which businesses are visible, which products get recommended, which provider gets the order. Whoever owns the agent layer owns the entire commercial relationship. That is not a technical question. It is an economic and political one. And it is the question this post is about.

This was written two days after Anthropic launched managed agents. Some of it will turn out to be wrong. I'll follow up with corrections as the picture becomes clearer.

The pattern

We have watched this before. A US technology company builds a platform so useful that it becomes the default intermediary between businesses and customers. Amazon did it with retail. Google did it with search. Facebook did it with social. In each case, the platform started as infrastructure, became a marketplace, and ended up owning the customer relationship, the data, and the economics. Local businesses became tenants. Local markets became afterthoughts.

Two days ago, Anthropic launched managed agents. It is excellent engineering: a three-way decoupled architecture with crash-resilient sessions, hardened orchestration, and context engineering that represents real research. For enterprise deployments, it sets a new standard.

But if every device becomes a prompt window, managed agents is not just an orchestration product. It is a bid to become the operating system of all commerce. The model, the orchestrator, the execution environment, the session store, the credential vault, and the customer relationship, all bundled into one offering from one US company. The domain-specific tools that make it useful for specific markets? Those are expected from the community, for free.

Five layers of AI-commerce

To understand what is at stake, it helps to see what AI-commerce actually requires. There are five distinct layers. Who owns each one determines who captures the value. But the answer is different depending on whether the product serves an enterprise or a consumer.

Enterprise products

Rakuten deployed agents across five departments in a week. Sentry shipped a bug-fix agent with a single engineer. These are enterprise products where the business already has its own infrastructure, its own customer base, and its own tools. Managed agents is the correct choice for the orchestrator layer here. The enterprise owns everything else.

Layer Who owns it Managed agents' role
Channels The enterprise (their app, their portal, their API) Not involved
Orchestrator Anthropic (managed agents) This is the product — hardened harness, crash recovery, context engineering
Model Anthropic (Claude) Bundled with orchestrator. Model lock-in is an accepted trade-off for reliability
Tools The enterprise (their internal MCP servers) Consumes tools via MCP. Enterprise retains ownership
Retailer / data integration The enterprise (their own systems) Not involved

This works. The enterprise hands the orchestrator to Anthropic and keeps everything else. The lock-in risk is real but manageable: enterprises have contracts, negotiating power, and the resources to migrate if needed. Managed agents earns its place here.

Consumer products

Now consider a different product: an AI grocery assistant for Australian families. The user says "buy me Colgate toothpaste, cheapest option." The agent searches Woolworths, Coles, and Chemist Warehouse, compares prices, and adds the cheapest to the user's cart. The user manages everything from a dashboard: order history, spending, family preferences. They interact through WhatsApp or Discord, not a $20/month chat application. This is AI-commerce at the consumer level.

Layer Who should own it What managed agents offers
Channels Local developers (WhatsApp, Discord, Telegram, dashboard) Not included — build your own
Orchestrator Local developers (user auth, sessions, persistent data) Ephemeral sandbox — no persistent database, no user accounts, no uptime between sessions
Model Interchangeable (Claude, GPT, Gemini, open-source) Claude only. Model lock-in is a liability at consumer margins
Tools Independent developers (cross-retailer comparison, price search) Consumes tools via MCP. Developer retains ownership
Retailer integration Independent developers today, retailers eventually Not involved

Here, managed agents provides one layer out of five: model inference. The orchestrator is ephemeral, so the developer needs their own infrastructure for user data, authentication, and persistent sessions anyway. The channels are not included. The tools are the developer's. The model should be interchangeable because consumer margins cannot absorb lock-in risk. For consumer AI-commerce, managed agents is a component, not a foundation.

The takeaway

For enterprise, managed agents earns its place as the orchestrator. For consumer products, it provides model inference but not the persistent infrastructure that consumer products require: user accounts, data storage, channels, dashboards. A consumer AI-commerce product needs independent infrastructure that the developer owns, with the model as one interchangeable component inside it.

I built Gombwe as a proof of concept: a personal agent that orders groceries, compares prices across Woolworths and Coles, and manages family schedules from a home machine in Melbourne. It runs on Discord and Telegram, uses scripts for deterministic work, and calls the model only when reasoning is needed. It is small, but it demonstrates what the consumer architecture looks like when the developer owns every layer.

What local developers must own

The tables above show the gap. For consumer AI-commerce, four out of five layers belong to local developers. These are not optional extras. They are the layers that determine whether AI-commerce serves consumers or extracts from them.

The proprietary functions layer. No retailer will build a tool that tells the customer a competitor has a better price. No AI company will build Australian grocery comparison tools. The cross-retailer search, price comparison, and independent recommendation layer must be built by independent developers. It can run anywhere: inside managed agents, as a local MCP server, or as a hosted service. What matters is that it is built independently of both the model provider and the retailers.

The channels people actually use. Most people in Australia, let alone Zimbabwe or Nigeria, are not going to interact with AI through a $20/month chat application. They will use WhatsApp, Telegram, Discord, SMS. The channel layer that connects agents to the interfaces people already use does not exist inside managed agents. Local developers build this.

The user relationship. Order history, dietary preferences, budgets, family profiles, spending dashboards. This data must live in the developer's infrastructure, not the model provider's ephemeral session store. Managed agents' sandbox is torn down after each session: there is no persistent database, no user accounts, no uptime between sessions. Building a consumer product on it still requires your own servers, your own authentication, your own database. The model provider replaces one layer. The product is still yours to build, host, and own.

The zero-cost path. Searching a retailer's website is an HTTP request. Comparing prices is a sort. Adding to cart is a form submission. These are deterministic operations that cost nothing to execute. An architecture that routes every action through paid model inference burns money on work that does not require intelligence. Local developers can build systems where the model is called only when reasoning is needed, and scripts handle the rest for free.

Model portability. Claude leads today. OpenAI, Google, and open-source alternatives are months behind at most. If a better model arrives next quarter, the tools, the channels, and the customer relationships should be portable. A model-agnostic architecture treats the model as a utility: call Claude for complex reasoning, a cheaper model for simple confirmations, an open-source model for zero-cost work. The tools, the channels, the user data, and the dashboard remain yours regardless of which model you call. Any business built entirely on one vendor's managed environment faces a migration, not a config change, when the market shifts.

Digital sovereignty

Managed agents runs on US infrastructure. For Australian customers, that means personal data, purchase history, and financial details processed offshore. For African markets, it is the same pattern that has played out with every previous platform: data extracted, value captured elsewhere.

This is not a theoretical concern. Europe is already moving away from US-owned collaboration platforms for sovereignty and independence reasons. The same AI companies building agent infrastructure are also defence contractors with national security obligations to their home governments. When the infrastructure that mediates all commerce in a country is owned, operated, and governed by a foreign company with defence ties, that is not a technology choice. It is a sovereignty decision.

Building good models does not entitle a company to own the commerce layer of every country those models reach. The tools, the channels, and the domain logic that connect AI to local businesses should be built by local developers, hosted in local jurisdictions, and governed by local rules. AI-commerce is too important to be surrendered to a single country because that country builds good models. Models will commoditise. The commercial layer that sits on top of them is where the lasting value lives, and that layer should belong to the markets it serves.

When an Australian family orders groceries through an AI agent, the purchase data, the dietary preferences, the spending patterns, and the commercial margins should stay in Australia, governed by Australian law, benefiting Australian businesses and developers. The same principle applies to every country where AI-commerce takes root.

What we are building

At Agents Formation in Melbourne, we are building the layers that neither the model providers nor the retailers will build:

  • The Australian MCP Registry: open-source, model-agnostic tools for Australian grocery, starting with Woolworths and Coles price comparison and ordering
  • Gombwe: an open-source personal agent that lives in Discord and Telegram, orders groceries, manages family schedules, and runs on a home machine using an existing subscription
  • Channel adapters for WhatsApp, Telegram, and Discord, so end users never need to know about AI subscriptions
  • A dashboard for users to manage their agent interactions, order history, spending, and preferences

These tools are model-agnostic. They work with Claude today and will work with any model tomorrow. The architecture separates each layer so that the model can be swapped without touching the tools, the channels, or the user data.

A call to action

To Australian developers: the tools layer for AI-commerce in this country is unbuilt. Every domain, grocery, energy, insurance, travel, needs independent, cross-provider comparison tools that work in the customer's interest. Build them as MCP servers. Make them model-agnostic. Own the layer that the model providers will never build and the incumbents have no incentive to build fairly.

To Australian businesses: in AI-commerce, the businesses that are agent-accessible get customers. The ones that are not become invisible. Publishing an MCP endpoint is the equivalent of building a website in 1999. The retailers and service providers who do it first will be discoverable by every AI agent. The ones who wait will lose share to those who did not.

To policymakers: AI-commerce will determine who captures the economic value of consumer transactions in the next decade. If the orchestration layer, the user data, and the commercial tools are all owned by US model providers, Australian businesses become tenants again, the same way they became tenants of Amazon, Google, and Facebook. Policy should encourage local AI-commerce infrastructure: local hosting, local data governance, local tool development, and model portability so that no single foreign provider becomes a gatekeeper to Australian commerce.

Managed agents is excellent engineering. Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, and others will continue to build better models and better infrastructure. That is welcome. But the history of platform economics tells us that the layers between businesses and customers are too important to be owned by one company in one country. The model providers are not oblivious to this — they are simply building for a different problem. It falls to local developers, local businesses, and local policymakers to ensure that AI-commerce infrastructure is built locally, owned locally, and governed locally. The model is a utility. The value is in the layers around it. Those layers should belong to the developers, the businesses, and the countries they serve.
